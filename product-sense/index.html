Before You Begin

What This Document Is & Who It's For

Product sense & design is the compass. A PM with strong execution but weak product sense ships the wrong thing fast. A founder with strong network & capital but weak product sense builds toward the wrong destination with plenty of runway. A solo builder who can now prototype, launch, and iterate in days - but can't tell you which user they're solving for or why that user would choose them over the next app shipped tomorrow - will join the 95% of products that fail with perfectly functional code.

AI collapsed the cost of building to near zero. That made product sense more valuable, not less - because when everyone can build, the only advantage left is identifying what's worth building. This document breaks it into seven phases, six levels, and one case study that shows exactly what separates each level from the next.

Phase 3 - Choose Your User
Bottom 80%

"Main segments: millennials who want to save, families on a budget, and high earners who overspend. I'll focus on millennials since they're the largest group and most likely to adopt a new app."

Top 10%

"Focusing on 'irregular-income earners' - gig workers, freelancers, seasonal workers - who have downloaded and abandoned 3+ budgeting apps. Every app failed them because budgets assume a predictable paycheck. Their transaction data reveals exactly when and why each previous app stopped being useful. ~40% of US workers have some form of irregular income. No product is designed for their cash flow pattern."

The gap: Bottom 80% segments by demographics - an axis that tells you nothing about why people fail at money management. Top 10% segments by behavioral pattern and identifies a structural mismatch (variable income vs. fixed budgets) that explains why every existing app has failed this group.

Phase 4 - Identify the Real Pain
Bottom 80%

"Three main pain points: people don't track spending, need better spending categories, and want automated categorization. Budgeting is tedious and takes too much effort."

Top 10%

"Low engagement and poor financial outcomes look like separate problems. They share one root cause: the app's first meaningful screen shows users how much they overspent. That screen triggers shame - and shame makes users stop opening the app. The product's core design choice - showing raw spending data as the entry experience - produces the avoidance it was designed to prevent. Every competitor addresses this by softening the data (gentler labels, rounded numbers). None address it by changing what the user sees first."

The gap: Bottom 80% lists symptoms every budgeting app already addresses. Top 10% identifies the mechanism producing those symptoms - the product's own entry experience creates the avoidance every competitor is trying to fix.

Phase 5 - Design Solutions
Bottom 80%

"Spending tracker with categories and alerts. A savings goal with a progress bar. Budget alerts when overspending. Weekly spending summary email."

Top 10%

"Horizon 1: 'Financial check-in' - a 60-second weekly summary that shows one positive pattern before the full picture. Reframes the entry from audit to progress. Ships in 4 weeks. Starts collecting data on which financial framings produce the highest voluntary return rates. Horizon 2: Predictive cash flow for irregular earners using transaction pattern data - only possible with accumulated history. Horizon 3: Embedded financial products (auto-savings, credit-building) that activate at the specific behavioral moment the user is most receptive."

The gap: Bottom 80% lists features that exist in 500 budgeting apps - any competitor already has them. Top 10% designs a compounding sequence where each horizon generates the data the next one requires, built on transaction pattern assets no new entrant can replicate.

Two Questions That Anchor the Entire Argument

"Why does solving this problem, for this company or venture, strategically matter right now?"
This determines which user segment matters, which goal to optimize, and which solutions are actually buildable given your resources and position. An answer without this anchor has no way to justify why it chose one direction over another. For founders, this is existential: the wrong answer burns runway solving a problem the market doesn't care about yet.
"Who is the most underserved user - and why has no one solved it yet?"
Not the largest segment. The user whose gap between need and current solution is widest - and where this company has a specific advantage in closing it. This choice cascades into everything: which pains matter, which solutions are relevant, and which metrics measure the right thing.

1

Clarify

Surface the questions and constraints that change your direction - before generating a single idea

Product problems arrive ambiguous. A brief that says "improve retention" doesn't specify which retention problem, for which user, or what business constraints bound the solution space. The goal of this phase: identify what's actually being asked, name the constraints that eliminate wrong directions, and confirm the scope before committing. The test for every clarification: would a different answer change what you build? If not, the clarification is ritual.

Why this phase matters differently by contextFor PMs and aspiring PMs, this is where interviewers decide whether your clarification was ritual or real - and where product reviews distinguish between someone who scopes work and someone who reframes problems. For founders, this is where you catch the scope error that burns 6 months of runway. The founder who never questions "improve retention" builds retention features. The founder who asks "is the problem really retention, or is it that the product solves the wrong problem?" might pivot before it's too late.
1.1Questions & Constraints That Change Your Direction

What it measures: Do you surface the real question and the real constraints before starting - and do they actually change your product direction?

Case Study"Build a product that helps people manage their money better" - What do you clarify before starting, and how does it change your direction?
LevelThinking & BehaviorExampleWhat it takes to move up
Bottom 80% You ask a clarifying question or state a constraint, but neither changes your direction. The clarification is ritual - it signals awareness of structure without doing anything with it. "I'll build a budgeting app for millennials on mobile." Proceeds with the same analysis regardless. The constraint was stated, not used - a different platform or demographic would produce the identical answer. Before asking any question or stating any constraint, check: would a different answer actually change your next step? If not, the question is ritual. Only clarify things whose answers produce different product directions.
Top 50% You ask one real clarifying question or use a constraint to narrow scope - and genuinely adjust based on it. The clarification narrows work but doesn't tell you what to build. "Are we building for people who don't budget at all, or people who've tried budgeting and quit? Both are money-management problems but they require different products." Waits for the answer and adjusts. "12-month timeline means no banking license - I'll scope out lending features." Both are real, but neither reveals a direction - they only reduce scope. Ask: does this clarification tell you what to build, or just what to cut? A scope question or timeline constraint narrows work - useful, but not insight. The next level uses clarification to identify which direction is right, not just which directions to remove.
Top 20% You use the business context - revenue model, competitive position, or strategic priorities - to test a hypothesis and eliminate entire solution categories before generating ideas. "This prompt spans at least three different products: a spending tracker (Mint), a budgeting system (YNAB), and a financial planning tool (Wealthfront). The business model constraint eliminates most: ads require massive scale, subscription requires retention, financial product referrals require trust built over months. A subscription model means the product must deliver enough value in month 1 to justify $10/month - that eliminates anything requiring 6 months of data to become useful." The question reveals prior thinking about the business situation. Study the business model before engaging. For PMs: what is this company's biggest business problem this year? For founders: what does your runway, team size, or distribution force you to focus on? The constraint most people ignore is often the one that explains why existing solutions have failed.
Top 10% You identify a tension or constraint that, if unresolved, produces a wrong answer regardless of execution quality. Your clarification reframes what the problem actually is. "500 budgeting apps exist. Most people still can't budget. That means the problem is not missing tools. The real barrier: most people avoid looking at their finances because it makes them feel bad. Any product framed as 'a better budgeting app' inherits this avoidance. The clarification that changes everything: are we building a financial tool, or are we solving the reason people refuse to use financial tools?" The question isn't about scope - it's about whether the standard approach is even correct. Practice asking: "What would make this problem hard to solve even with perfect execution?" The answer is usually a tension hidden inside the prompt - two goals that conflict, or a constraint everyone treats as fixed that might be the actual problem.
Top 1% You identify an embedded constraint - an existing investment, organizational commitment, or market structure - that everyone treats as fixed, and show that designing with it (rather than against it) produces a faster, more shippable path to value. "The most important constraint is regulatory: any product touching financial data must comply with open banking regulations (PSD2 in EU, Section 1033 in US). Most builders treat this as a cost. It's actually the moat: open banking gives you real-time transaction access that manual-entry apps can't match, and the compliance barrier eliminates low-effort competitors. A 3-person team can build on Plaid's API in 6 weeks. But a competitor who wants to replicate your transaction-pattern model needs 12+ months of user data you've already accumulated. The regulatory constraint you'd instinctively descope is the thing that makes your data advantage compound." Identify one constraint in any product brief and ask: "Is this actually fixed, or is it a scoping assumption someone made and never questioned?" For founders: which constraint, if leaned into rather than avoided, becomes your defensible advantage over time?
Top 0.1% You identify that the prompt contains a category error - the product is optimizing the wrong metric entirely - and your clarification reframes the problem at the business-model level. You can quantify the competitive distance this reframe creates. "'Help people manage their money better' assumes the problem is individual behavior. But most financial stress comes from structural mismatches: income arrives monthly, bills arrive irregularly; income is variable, budgets are fixed; income is deposited in one account, spending happens across five. The product that actually works doesn't help people manage money better within the current structure - it restructures how money flows to match how life actually works. That's not a feature - it's a business-model-level decision about whether you're building a visibility tool (analytics) or a flow-management tool (infrastructure). These are different companies competing in different markets." -
The shift to practice Pick any product brief, interview prompt, or business problem. Before generating ideas, write down every clarifying question you'd ask. Then test each one: "If the answer were different, would my next step change?" Cross out every question where the answer is no. The questions that survive are your real clarifications - the ones that change direction, not the ones that signal structure. For founders: rewrite your constraints (team size, runway, distribution) as forcing functions. A 6-person team, 18 months of runway, and no existing distribution aren't obstacles. They're the reason your solution must be focused enough to win a specific user before a funded competitor can.
2

Set Strategic Context

Understand why this problem is worth solving right now - and set a goal that directs every downstream choice

Phase 1 scoped the question. This phase anchors the answer in the actual position: for a company - its mission, defensible assets, competitive landscape, and current stage. For a founder - what market gap you've identified, what unfair advantage you bring, what timing makes this viable now, and how much runway you have to prove it. The output is a strategic goal - not a metric yet, but a directional claim about what to optimize for and why. Every choice from user segmentation through solution design flows from this claim.

Why this phase matters differently by contextFor PMs, this is where you demonstrate you understand the company's business well enough to set the right goal - not the obvious one. For founders, this is existential: the strategic context IS your founding thesis. Why this problem, why now, why you? If you can't answer all three with specifics, you're building on hope instead of insight. The quality of your Phase 2 answer predicts whether you'll still have runway in 12 months.
2.1Strategic Context - Why This, Why Now

What it measures: Can you determine whether this problem is worth solving right now - and if so, why you or your company are the right ones to solve it?

Case Study"Build a product that helps people manage their money better" - What is the strategic context?
LevelThinking & BehaviorExampleWhat it takes to move up
Bottom 80% The product in the prompt could be replaced by any competitor and the answer would be identical. No specific knowledge of this product's position, assets, or market timing shapes the direction. "Personal finance is a large and growing market. People need help managing their money." This is the same answer you'd give for Mint, YNAB, or any budgeting app. Nothing about a specific data asset, market timing, or behavioral insight changes the direction. After drafting any answer, replace the company name with a competitor. If the answer still works, you haven't used the company's specific context. Identify what would change if it were actually the competitor.
Top 50% You reference the most obvious asset (brand, user base, or data) once. It appears in the answer but doesn't fundamentally change the design direction. "The personal finance app market is worth $X billion. Mobile-first is the right platform because users check their phones 90 times/day." The market is mentioned but the solution direction - mobile budgeting - is the same one any finance app would build. The context is decorating a generic answer. After naming the company's context, check: does this context change which problem you'd focus on? If the same context paragraph could describe a competitor, it hasn't shaped your direction yet.
Top 20% You connect the current business situation to the design goal. The strategic context tells you which user segment matters and which metric to optimize - and that answer would be different for a different product in the same space. "Open banking APIs (Plaid, TrueLayer) became reliable and affordable in 2020-2022. Before this, budgeting apps required users to manually enter transactions or share bank credentials. Manual entry has 90%+ abandonment in week 1. Open banking solved the data input problem - but no one has solved the data interpretation problem for irregular earners. The timing: build on the infrastructure layer before banks build their own consumer tools on the same APIs." Before engaging with any product problem, research: what is the biggest business problem this year? What competitor move is most threatening? For PMs: read the last two earnings reports or recent analyst coverage. For founders: what did the last 3 competitors in this space get wrong, and what market shift makes your timing different? This context should change your answer meaningfully.
Top 10% You identify the specific external force that makes this problem urgent right now - and can explain the timing mechanism: what condition became true this year that wasn't true before, and what happens if the company doesn't act within a specific window. "Two things became true in the last 2 years. Open banking APIs gave any developer real-time access to user transaction data. Generative AI made it possible to interpret those transactions in plain language. The window: 12-18 months before major banks ship their own tools on the same APIs. The defensible asset is not the AI - that's commoditizing fast. The defensible asset is behavioral data: which messages change spending behavior for which types of users. That data takes months of real users to build. Every month of head start widens the gap." Identify: "What external event - competitor move, technology shift, regulatory change - made this problem urgent this year? What is the window of opportunity, and what happens if they miss it?" If you can't name the event and the deadline, you haven't identified the strategic context.
Top 1% You identify an underutilized asset (proprietary data, distribution, infrastructure, or for founders: a unique insight or access advantage), quantify the competitive distance it creates, and design a product direction that only works because of that asset - where the competitive gap widens with scale rather than narrows. "Every finance app has transaction data. The untapped asset is intervention-response data: how does this user's spending change when shown a progress message vs. a deficit warning? Which framing reduces impulse purchases for anxious spenders vs. optimistic ones? No product has systematically tested this. 12 months of A/B-tested interventions across 100K users builds a dataset a new competitor would need 2+ years to replicate. Each user who stays generates more data, making the next user's experience more personalized. The gap widens with scale." Build an asset inventory: proprietary data, distribution advantages, technology infrastructure, brand trust, ecosystem relationships. The strongest strategic contexts identify which of these assets are underutilized - capabilities the company has but hasn’t built against.
Top 0.1% You identify that the current approach frames the problem wrong - and the right strategic move requires a business-model-level decision, not a product-level feature. For an established product, the reframe changes resource allocation, success metrics, and market positioning for 3-5 years. For a founder, this IS the founding insight: seeing that the entire category has been solving the wrong problem. "The personal finance category has been framed wrong for 30 years. Every product is built as a visibility tool: show people where their money goes and they'll make better decisions. This is factually wrong - behavioral economics has demonstrated that financial visibility without structural change produces guilt without behavior change in the majority of users. The right strategic move isn't building a better visibility tool. It's building financial flow infrastructure: a product that automatically routes money to the right place at the right time based on the user's actual income pattern. Visibility tools compete with 500 apps. Flow infrastructure competes with banking rails. Different market, different revenue model, different competitive set entirely." -
The shift to practice Take any product answer you've written and replace the company name with a competitor's. If the answer still works, you haven't used strategic context - you've decorated a generic answer. Redo it with one specific constraint: what asset, timing, or market position would change if this were the competitor instead? For founders: answer three questions with specifics, not abstractions. Why is the market ready now when it wasn't 2 years ago? Why can you specifically win (your unfair advantage)? What have competitors tried and failed at - and what did they get wrong?
2.2Goal Setting & North Star Metric

What it measures: Do you set a specific, measurable goal - anchored by a North Star metric - that filters every downstream decision and aligns user value with business value?

Case Study"Build a product that helps people manage their money better" - What goal and North Star metric do you set?
LevelThinking & BehaviorExampleWhat it takes to move up
Bottom 80% Goal is aspirational, not actionable. You cannot use it to say no to anything. "The goal is to help people save more money." Zero filtering power - any feature can be justified against this. After naming a goal, ask: "Can I use this goal to say no to a proposed solution?" If every solution you might propose also improves this goal, the goal is too broad. A useful goal eliminates at least 30% of solution candidates immediately.
Top 50% Goal is a specific AARRR stage. You know which funnel stage you're targeting, but the reason for choosing it is vague or unstated. "I'll optimize for monthly active users." Better - specific and measurable. But why MAU over retention, revenue, or savings rate? Without a business case for why this metric over others, the choice is arbitrary. Every goal needs a business case: "I'm targeting [metric] because the current industry number is [X], the gap to [Y] represents [Z] in premium conversion value, and [this metric] specifically captures [mechanism]."
Top 20% Goal is specific and justified with a business case. You chose this metric because you know where the biggest opportunity is. "Goal: increase the % of users who stay within their self-set budget for 3 consecutive months. Current industry baseline: ~12% of budgeting app users achieve this. Target: 25%. This metric directly measures whether the product is working - users who hit budget 3 months running convert to premium at 4x the rate of users who don't." Add user-business alignment: state explicitly that the user behavior improving this metric is also good for the user, not just the business. If you can't make this case, the metric is probably optimizing at the user's expense.
Top 10% Goal explicitly aligns user value with business value - and you name this alignment to rule out solutions that optimize the metric at the user's expense. "Goal: increase voluntary financial check-ins - users who open the app without a notification prompting them. A user who checks on their own is checking because the experience is useful, not because an alert made them anxious. This metric aligns user value with business value: notification-driven check-ins correlate with anxiety, not better financial decisions. Voluntary check-ins correlate with actual behavior change. The metric can only improve if the product feels worth opening." Identify the tension: for a finance app, engagement can come from anxiety (overspending alerts) or from genuine confidence (financial progress evidence). Name this tension explicitly, take a position on which to optimize, and explain why.
Top 1% You name the tension between the right user goal and the right business goal - and make an explicit choice about which takes priority and why. "Financial confidence and engagement can pull in opposite directions. If the product makes users feel good about their finances, they may stop checking - reducing engagement even though the product is working. The goal that resolves this: '% of users whose actual spending variance decreases month-over-month.' This metric only improves when real behavior changes - not when users feel better about the same behavior. Lower variance means less financial stress for the user and more predictable data for the business. Both sides win on the same number." Study one company per week where the right user metric and the right business metric appear to conflict. Identify the resolution: when user success and business success diverge, it's almost always a sign that the business metric is being measured too short-term.
Top 0.1% You identify that the stated goal is a proxy metric - one being optimized because it's measurable, not because it's right - and propose a better one. This applies whether you're questioning a company's existing metric or challenging the default metric an entire category uses. "The industry measures 'savings rate' as the North Star. This is a proxy - it measures the outcome of financial management, not the behavior that produces it. The right North Star is 'financial decision confidence' - does the user feel equipped to make their next financial decision without anxiety? This is currently unmeasured by any product. The company that measures it can enter the $50B financial advisory market with a behavioral data moat: they know which interventions produce financial confidence in which user profiles. No robo-advisor or bank has this data because none of them measure the behavioral precursor to good financial outcomes." -
Does your goal pass these three tests?
  1. Specific enough to say no - "improve retention" passes everything through. "Increase D30 retention for users who've shown real intent but haven't built a durable habit" eliminates 60% of potential solutions immediately.
  2. Tied to the business context - "why this goal, not another?" has a clear answer rooted in the current strategic position - the company's situation for PMs, the market gap and timing for founders.
  3. Falsifiable - you can tell within a defined timeframe whether you've achieved it.
Does your North Star metric pass these five tests?
  1. Measures customer value - captures when users achieve their "aha moment" (e.g., Airbnb's nights booked, not page views).
  2. Connects to the goal - directly tied to the strategic goal above, not a generic engagement number.
  3. Leading indicator - predicts future revenue or retention, not just reports on the past.
  4. Actionable - the team can influence it through product decisions.
  5. Clear and focused - simple enough to align the entire team.

The metric must capture the goal, not approximate it: if the goal is habit formation and the metric is DAU, you're measuring attendance, not habit. The full supporting metrics architecture (input metrics, guardrails, business outcomes) comes in Phase 6.

3

Choose Your User

Find the most underserved segment and justify the choice across explicit criteria

Segmentation is the most differentiating skill in product sense. Almost every PM lists user types. Very few choose the right segmentation axis and find the segment with the highest unmet need. For founders, this choice is even higher stakes - a PM who picks the wrong segment misses a quarter; a founder who picks the wrong segment burns 6-12 months of runway building for users who don't exist in sufficient numbers or won't pay.

3.1Choosing the Right Segmentation Axis

What it measures: Do you segment by behavioral motivation and relationship with the problem - or by demographics that produce groups with identical product needs?

Case Study"Build a product that helps people manage their money better" - What axis do you use to segment users?
LevelThinking & BehaviorExampleWhat it takes to move up
Bottom 80% You segment by demographics or proficiency level. The groups are socially distinct but their product needs are the same. "Users by demographics: millennials, Gen Z, families, retirees." These groups all face the same core problem - difficulty managing money - and churn from budgeting apps for the same reasons. The segmentation generates no different product directions. Replace every demographic descriptor ("millennial saver") with a behavioral one ("user who downloaded 3 budgeting apps and abandoned each within 30 days"). Demographic labels describe who; behavioral labels predict what they need.
Top 50% You segment by use case. Groups have different feature needs, but the axis focuses on what they do rather than why they stay or leave. "Users by financial behavior: savers, spenders, and avoiders." Better - these groups have different relationships with money. But the axis still describes observable behavior, not the psychological mechanism that drives each pattern. For any segment, ask: "What is the specific decision this person faces, and what stops them from making the right one?" The answer to that question is your behavioral segmentation axis.
Top 20% You segment by motivation and relationship with the barrier. Different motivations produce fundamentally different products. "Users cluster by their relationship with financial visibility:
(1) data-driven optimizers - users who check daily and enjoy it (don't need this product),
(2) anxiety-driven checkers - users who check because they're worried, not because they find it useful (need the product to reduce anxiety, not increase information),
(3) avoiders - users who know they should check but feel so ashamed they don't (the 60-70% who download and abandon),
(4) structurally excluded - people whose income pattern makes standard budgeting impossible (need a fundamentally different model).

These groups need four completely different products."		 After segmenting by motivation, verify: "If I gave two users in the same segment the same product, would both find it essential?" If not, the segment is still too broad - there's a second behavioral axis inside it.
Top 10% You find the segmentation axis your competitors use, identify its flaw, and propose a better one that reveals an underserved group. "Every finance app segments by income level or spending behavior. The right axis is 'relationship with financial shame':
(1) Confident - feel in control, don't need this product.
(2) Guilty but engaged - current budgeting app users who check despite discomfort.
(3) Avoiders - downloaded the app, saw their spending, felt shame, stopped opening it. This is 60-70% of all budgeting app users.
(4) Structurally excluded - built elaborate systems to avoid seeing their finances at all (separate accounts, cash-only spending, refusing to check balances).

Define Success Metrics

Build the supporting metrics architecture around the North Star - input metrics, business outcomes, and guardrails that prevent gaming

The North Star was set in Phase 2. This phase builds the supporting system around it: the input metrics the team moves week-to-week, the business outcomes the North Star predicts, and the guardrails that prevent optimizing at the user's expense. For founders with 50 users instead of 50M: the architecture is the same, but the signals are different. At small scale, you're looking for qualitative leading indicators (do users come back unprompted? do they tell others?) that predict what the quantitative metrics will show at scale. 3-layer supporting architecture:
(1) Input metrics - user behaviors that drive the North Star.
(2) Business outcomes - downstream results the North Star predicts.
(3) Guardrails - metrics that must not degrade as inputs improve.

Habits Applied in Every Phase

4 Habits That Instantly Upgrade Your Product Sense & Design

These run through every phase. A PM, founder, or builder who applies them consistently will score one level higher on the same content - in an interview, a product review, a pitch, or a build decision.

1 - Explain Why Before the What

At every phase, explain your reasoning before stating your conclusion. Not "I'll focus on financial avoiders" but "60-70% of budgeting app users abandon within 90 days because the first meaningful data view triggers shame, not insight - I'll focus on this segment because the entry experience is the highest-leverage intervention point." The mechanism is what separates a defensible choice from an arbitrary one.

The test: After every statement, ask "did I explain why before the what?" If you stated a conclusion without naming the mechanism, the listener cannot evaluate whether your logic is sound - only whether your conclusion sounds reasonable.

Without mechanism"I'd focus on users who avoid checking their finances."
With mechanism"Roughly 60-70% of budgeting app users abandon within 30 days. The mechanism isn't boredom or missing features - it's that the first meaningful data view (week 3, when spending patterns emerge) delivers a negative emotional surprise. Users who see they exceeded their self-set budget in the first full month have a 60% permanent abandonment rate. The avoidance moment after the first negative data view is the highest-leverage intervention point - so I'd focus on redesigning that specific experience."

2 - Anchor Every Claim to a Number

Every claim is more credible with an order-of-magnitude number attached. Not precise data - rough estimates that show you're thinking quantitatively. If you can't attach any number to any claim, you're operating at a level of abstraction too high to drive real decisions.

What needs a number: segment size ("roughly 40% of active users"), pain frequency ("happens 2-3 times per week"), solution impact ("could affect 30-40% of new users"), metric target ("from 18% to 25% D30 retention").

When you don't have exact data: estimate out loud. "I don't have exact numbers, but rough order of magnitude: if 60% of a 20M-user app's base has abandoned within 90 days, that's 12M users in the recoverable window. Even a 10% improvement in that cohort's return rate is 1.2M users." What separates strong from junior is not having the exact number - it's having the habit of estimating rather than speaking in vague magnitudes. "A large portion" tells the listener nothing. "Roughly 40%" tells them your thinking is quantitative even when the precise figure isn't known.

Without number"A lot of people struggle with financial anxiety from budgeting apps."
With number"Roughly 60-70% of budgeting app users abandon within 90 days. At Mint's scale of ~20M users, that's 12-14M people who tried financial management and concluded they can't do it. Even a 10% improvement in that cohort's retention is 1.2-1.4M users - a user base larger than most fintech startups' total."

3 - Prioritize with Explicit Criteria, Then Name the Trade-off

When you say "I'll focus on X because..." and give one reason, you've made a preference call. When you score each option against 4 criteria, you've made a business decision. The criteria make your reasoning auditable - a challenge targets a specific weight, not your overall judgment. After scoring, always name what you chose not to do and why accepting that cost is right.

The four criteria for any prioritization choice (segment, pain, or solution):
(1) Severity of unmet need - where is the gap between current solution and actual need widest?
(2) Defensible advantage - is there a specific asset (data, distribution, or infrastructure) that makes this product better positioned here?
(3) Strategic alignment - does this advance the goal set in Phase 2?
(4) Flywheel effect - does winning here improve the product for other segments?

4 - Hold Your Position Under Challenge

When challenged - by an investor, a co-founder, a teammate, a customer, or an interviewer - the instinct is to concede. Don't. The challenge is testing whether you have conviction behind your choices. Conceding without engaging is worse than defending a wrong answer - defending with reasoning shows you can think through pressure. Pure concession shows you can't hold a position.

The protocol:
(1) Acknowledge: "That's a fair point."
(2) State your reasoning: "My logic was [X]."
(3) Evaluate whether the challenge changes the reasoning: "If [the challenged assumption] is true, I'd adjust by [Y]. But I think [original assumption] is more likely because [specific reason]."

ConcedingChallenge: "Wouldn't regular-income users be a better segment - there are more of them and the product is simpler?" PM: "You're right, let's focus on regular-income users instead." Position reversed with no reasoning - shows the position had no analytical foundation.
Holding with engagement"That's fair - regular-income users have simpler needs and faster activation. My position: irregular-income earners generate 3x the transaction data per user because of multiple income sources. This data trains the prediction model on hard cases, which improves accuracy for easy cases. If the data showed regular-income users generated equivalent prediction value, I'd change my answer. But given the data asymmetry, the irregular-income segment is the right starting point - and the prediction model built for them benefits everyone."
