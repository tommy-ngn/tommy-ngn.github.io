Product Sense & Design

AI enables building more cheaply and rapidly. The bottleneck is now knowing what's worth building. This is a structured system for answering that question — from clarifying the real problem to designing solutions that compound.

The Problem This Solves

Most product thinking follows the same pattern: jump to solutions before understanding the problem, segment users by demographics instead of behavior, treat "I talked to 5 users" as validation, and confuse shipping features with creating value.

The result is predictable. 95% of new products fail. Not because the teams couldn't build — but because they built the wrong thing for the wrong user for the wrong reason. AI just made this 10x more urgent. When anyone can build anything in a weekend, the gap between what succeeds and what doesn't is no longer technical. It's product sense.

The Seven Phases

  1. Clarify

    Before generating any ideas, identify what's actually being asked and which constraints change the direction. Find the constraint everyone treats as fixed and show that designing with it — not against it — produces a defensible advantage. Ask: "What would make this problem unsolvable even with perfect execution?" The answer reveals the hidden tension in the prompt.

    The move most people miss: The product is framed at the wrong level of abstraction. "Help people manage money better" assumes individual behavior is the problem. Most financial stress is structural — income and bills arrive on mismatched schedules. The real choice isn't "which features?" — it's "are we building analytics or infrastructure?" Different companies, different markets.

  2. Set Strategic Context

    Anchor in the actual position — what assets exist, what timing makes this urgent, what competitors have tried and failed at. Build an asset inventory: proprietary data, distribution, infrastructure, brand trust, ecosystem. Which is underutilized?

    The move most people miss: Identify timing — "What became true this year that wasn't true 2 years ago?" Design toward the asset that creates a widening competitive gap, where each user makes the next user's experience better. If your strategic context could describe any competitor, you haven't done this phase.

  3. Choose Your User

    Find the most underserved segment and justify the choice across explicit criteria. Never segment by demographics as the primary axis. Segment by motivation, relationship with the barrier, or behavioral pattern — dimensions that predict what different users need.

    The move most people miss: Look for the segment competitors actively avoid — "too hard," "not our core user." That's often the highest-value unmet need. Every budgeting app targets people who want to budget. The underserved segment: people who tried budgeting 3+ times and quit. Highest willingness to pay, deepest distrust of budget-app framing.

  4. Identify the Real Pain

    Get below symptoms to the mechanism producing the behavior. Trace three layers: Functional (what's broken), Emotional (how it feels), Identity (what the user believes about themselves when this happens). Every competitor addresses functional. Emotional and identity layers are where the gap sits.

    The move most people miss: Ask "What do users say they want?" vs. "What does their behavior show they need?" When stated want and behavioral need diverge, design for the behavioral need. If a competitor tried solving this directly and the pain persists — what assumption did their solution make that was wrong?

  5. Design Solutions

    Set the product vision in one sentence before generating solutions. Design solutions as a compounding sequence — Horizon 1 generates the data or behavior that Horizon 2 requires. If horizons are independent bets, they're a list, not a strategy.

    The move most people miss: "What behavior does every competitor reinforce? What happens if we do the opposite?" Structural inversions that competitors can't copy without abandoning their own architecture are the strongest moves. For Horizon 2+, the right question isn't "what features?" — it's "what organizational narrative will form around early data, and will it help or kill Horizon 2?"

  6. Define Success Metrics

    Build three layers with explicit causal links: Input metrics (what the team moves weekly) → North Star → Business outcomes (revenue, retention). State the mechanism connecting each layer. For every metric, ask: "What bad behavior could a team exhibit while improving this metric that harms users?"

    The move most people miss: The standard metric in your category is probably a proxy — optimized because it's measurable, not because it's right. "Is this measuring the outcome or the behavior that produces the outcome?" If your metric can improve while the product is actually failing, you're measuring a proxy.

  7. Risks & Mitigations

    Identify what could break and define thresholds that trigger action before damage compounds. Structure risks into MECE categories: User (experience quality), Engineering (performance, reliability), Business (cannibalization, opportunity cost, regulatory). Include opportunity cost: N months on this means no investment in what competitors are building.

    The move most people miss: "What metric could improve while the feature is actually failing?" That's your first risk indicator. Then: "What will the early data look like? What story will leadership tell about it? What damage will be invisible in that story?"

The Six Levels

Each phase can be executed at different depths. The framework distinguishes six levels of thinking quality — from generic answers anyone could give, to structural insights that reframe the entire problem.

  • Level 1 — Generic: Could apply to any product. No specificity.
  • Level 2 — Correct: Reasonable answer, standard thinking. Passes a quick review.
  • Level 3 — Good: Shows genuine product thinking. Identifies the obvious trade-offs.
  • Level 4 — Top 10%: Specific to this product, this user, this market. Numbers on every claim. Criteria before decisions.
  • Level 5 — Top 1%: Identifies what everyone else misses. Designs with constraints, not against them. Finds the compounding mechanism. Names the trade-off and why the cost is acceptable.
  • Level 6 — Top 0.1%: Reframes the problem at a different level of abstraction. Category errors identified. Business-model-level insights, not feature-level. Changes what question you're answering.

Cross-Cutting Rules

Four rules that raise output quality by one level, regardless of phase:

  1. Mechanism before conclusion. Never state a choice without the reasoning that produced it. Not "I'll focus on avoiders" but "[mechanism] — therefore I'll focus on avoiders."
  2. Numbers on every claim. Attach order-of-magnitude estimates: segment size, pain frequency, impact magnitude. Estimated is fine. Absent is not.
  3. Explicit criteria, then the trade-off. Score options against 3-4 criteria. After choosing, name what you're NOT doing and why the cost is acceptable.
  4. Hold position under challenge. Acknowledge the challenge, state your reasoning, evaluate whether it changes the analysis, and hold or adjust with stated logic.

Use This Framework

This framework is designed to work two ways:

  • As a thinking tool: Walk through the seven phases for any product decision — new feature, new product, or product improvement. The phases force you to answer the hard questions before committing resources.
  • As an AI skill: Drop the SKILL.md into Claude, ChatGPT, or any AI platform's project context. It turns the AI into a structured product thinking partner that operates at Top 1% as the target — not a generic chatbot that restates your question back to you.
